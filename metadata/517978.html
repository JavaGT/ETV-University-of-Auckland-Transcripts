etv/panel_media_and_pare_20240312_0122Panel: Media and Parenting Across Childhood79 views Dec 9, 2023 In this panel, members of the CAFE (Comprehensive Assessment of Family Media Exposure) Consortium presented research findings across early childhood, middle childhood, and adolescence that capture the complexity of media use, parent-child relationships, and the structural forces that shape child wellbeing and the digital environment. This panel was part of the 2023 Digital Media and Developing Minds International Scientific Congress on September 21, 2023. [0:00] Jenny Radesky, MD Jenny Radesky, MD, Professor of Pediatrics, Division Director, Developmental Behavioral Pediatrics at University of Michigan Medical School, provides an overview of the CAFE Consortium’s theoretical framework for studying and measuring parenting as it relates to screen time. Dr. Radesky outlines the importance of utilizing ecological and contextual models to understand media impacts more holistically and describes the novel Dynamic, Relational, Ecological Approach to Media Effects Research (DREAMER) framework. [13:33] Heather Kirkorian, PhD Heather Kirkorian, PhD, Professor of Human Development and Family Studies at University of Wisconsin-Madison summarizes Project Ecology of Media Use (EMU), discussing parental burnout and the ways in which it is related to parent and child media use, specifically in the context of infancy and early childhood. [28:14] Rachel Barr, PhD Rachel Barr, PhD, Professor of Psychology at Georgetown University, follows the DREAMER model to discuss research on the impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic on parenting around digital media use in middle childhood. Dr. Barr explores how the pandemic impacted overall media use as well as problematic media use in the US and globally. [39:33] Sarah Coyne, PhD Sarah Coyne, PhD, Professor of Human Development, School of Family Life at Brigham Young University, presents on the complicated relationships between parenting, digital media use, and mental health in adolescence. She explores potential roles of parent media use, parent-child relationship quality, technoference, and parental media mediation strategies.
